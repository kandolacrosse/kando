Learn Lacrosse (Properly)

For people who didn’t grow up playing. No American bias. No elite waffle. Just what actually helps at uni.

  • Attend an intro session
  • Observe the team culture
  • Ask questions freely

Even if you’ve played rugby or football before, lacrosse demands a completely different level of mobility and athleticism. Things like stamina, VO₂ max, hip flexibility, and wrist strength are crucial to thriving on the field. I started working on these straight after I began lacrosse, and it made all the difference. The earlier you start focusing on these, the faster you’ll feel comfortable and capable in sessions.

  • Borrow a stick if possible
  • Get the essential helmet/gloves
  • Wear trainers you can run in comfortably

You don’t need the top-end gear at first—just make sure you’re safe and comfortable. Borrowing sticks or pads from the club is perfect for the first few sessions. Wearing trainers that let you move, pivot, and sprint easily is way more important than looking flashy.

  • Start with wallballing religiously
  • Focus on catching & passing
  • Basic cradling and short-range shooting

Wallballing isn’t just a drill—it’s the foundation. I spent hours hitting the wall at home, and ever since then, every skill felt more natural. Catching and passing cleanly, cradling while moving, and shooting from close range all became far easier once I committed to wallballing. Treat it like your daily ritual; it pays off massively in your first few months.

  • Watch the flow of the game
  • Learn basic positions and responsibilities
  • Ask about formations and coverage

Even a few sessions of just observing scrimmages can give you a huge advantage. Asking a coach or an experienced teammate about positions and basic responsibilities saves so much time. Understanding where to be and when to move makes joining drills far less overwhelming.

  • Chat with teammates off the pitch
  • Attend club socials and group chats
  • Build confidence through connections

Lacrosse is just as much about the team environment as the sport itself. Making connections early—through socials, post-practice chats, or even casual messages—makes it easier to ask questions, join drills, and feel confident. It’s amazing how much being comfortable socially translates into performing better on the field.
